Falmouth Food Co-Op

Overview

A prototype mobile app for Falmouth Food Co-Op, a community-run online grocery store featuring local, non-industrial farmers. Designed to simplify the current ordering process. Users have expressed the existing site being confusing and difficult to place orders on, making them reluctant to purchase from.



Colour Palette

Simplified their existing colour palette. Bright colourful, reflecting the range of produce they sell and the range of people that they work with.


Empathy Map

Conducted an interview with someone who had never used FFC but would be interested. Gained some valuable insight into new customer bases thoughts & feelings.

User Flow

Evaluated the current FFC ordering process based on their website. Simplified the order process to be more user-friendly and more suitable for an app.

Dark Skies App

Overview

We believe everyone should get to experience stargazing.

Dark Skies is an app designed to make stargazing accessible for everyone by helping people discover safe, local dark sky locations.

This project was developed as part of my university's 'Start Up' module in a team of 5. My core role was UI & MVP designer utilising Figma.

The Problem

Finding a good dark sky spot shouldn't be this hard

Many people want to experience stargazing but can't because good dark sky spots are hard to find, in far out observatories, or hidden in word-of-mouth insider channels. This makes stargazing feel inaccessible for beginners and casuals.

The Insight

We wanted to understand what draws people to stargazing and what stops them

Before creating Dark Skies, we wanted to understand what makes stargazing both inspiring and difficult for people. Through competitor research, surveys, and a small focus group, we discovered a clear gap in local discovery.



Competitors

We compared two most of our most similar competitors: Dark Sky International and Go Stargazing.

  1. Dark Sky International: web-only, lists only officially recognised sites (21 in the UK)

  2. Go Stargazing: includes more sites, but relies on submissions by the official "Go Stargazing Team" and councils, meaning additions are slow.

Both lacked accessibility and immediate local coverage, showing a gap for a mobile-first, user-friendly option.



Survey

Our survey reached over 17 participants across our university campus as well as online stargazing communities

Most were casual stargazers, motivated by the awe and beauty of space & stars, rather than technical & complicated astronomy. They usually stargazed in easy to get to locations such as their gardens, beaches or just when walking their dog. Their biggest challenges were light pollution, cloud coverage and not being sure where to find better stargazing spots.



A/B & Hypothesis Testing

To identify which user need to prioritise, we ran small-scale A/B tests via Instagram ads comparing three core concepts:

  1. Social Need: planning group meetups
  2. Discovery Need: finding nearby dark sky zones
  3. Learning Need: helping beginners to start through resources & tutorials

These ads linked to a temporary page at darkskies.app, where users were directed to sign up to our newsletter for further information as well as letting us find interested people for a focus group.

In total we got...



The discovery need got the most clicks, showing the most user demand.



Focus Group

A follow up focus group, with some people from our newsletter signups as well as our survey, helped to reinforce this. Participants preferred discovering reliable local spots rather than planning meetups (they already had people they wanted to go with) and cited safety, time, and convenience as key concerns.

This validated our core problem statement: people want to stargaze, but finding safe, accessible local spots is too difficult.
